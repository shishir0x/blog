## How it started

This afternoon, while running a project, pnpm suddenly couldn't be found. After troubleshooting, I discovered that the Node v22 installation managed by fnm was incomplete, missing npm and corepack.

What did I do during this whole process? I threw the error at OpenClaw, it told me the reason, gave me the commands, and I copy-pasted and executed them. Done.

Then I froze for a moment — **What did I actually learn?**

Once this question popped up, I couldn't stop thinking about it. I chatted with AI for almost an hour, going from the pnpm error to AI dependence, learning methods, and the core value of human beings. Here is the summary and reflection after our chat.

---

## The Source of Anxiety

In the past, solving this kind of problem might have taken me an hour or two. Digging through documentation, searching Stack Overflow, trial and error. Although it was slow, I would always gain something each time: how environment variables work, what the PATH priority is, and the differences between PowerShell and cmd.

Now? One sentence, and AI handles it. Even when it can't, I just paste the new error back, it thinks again, and gives an answer. It took a dozen minutes back and forth, but my operation from start to finish was **copy and paste**.

For the same problem, figuring it out myself might take an hour or two, but the understanding would be much deeper.

I felt very conflicted. The thoughts were mine, but how much of the implementation process was mine? It felt like I only had the ideas left, and the execution relied entirely on AI. **So what is my core ability?**

---

## I Figured It Out A Bit

The old way of "being forced to learn by bugs" was actually **passive learning**. What you learned depended entirely on what bugs you encountered, which was very random.

What AI should replace is the time hijacked by these trivial problems. The time saved, if used to proactively learn something — systematically studying a field, reading source code, doing a side project — the depth achieved should actually be greater.

So the question is not "whether to use AI or not", but **what I did with the time saved**.

If the saved time is just used for scrolling on my phone, then I will indeed degrade. If it's used for deep diving, then it's a switch to a more efficient way of learning.

---

## So How Should We Learn?

When talking about learning methods, I mentioned bloggers I follow, like 3Blue1Brown and Karpathy.

After watching a 3Blue1Brown video, you get this "ah-ha" feeling; he turns abstract concepts into visuals you can see. This kind of **intuition** cannot be gained by reading text. Karpathy's "Let's build GPT" builds it step-by-step from scratch. After watching, it's not just "I know transformers", but "I can write one".

But watching videos and learning are two different things. Watching without getting your hands dirty is no different from scrolling short videos. **One video + one hands-on reproduction is more useful than scrolling through 20 videos.**

---

## What Abilities Are Valuable in the AI Era?

After today's chat, I increasingly feel that the following abilities have become even more important:

**Communication skills.** For the same solution, some people can talk for a long time and others still don't understand, while some can explain it clearly in three sentences. AI can help you write documentation, but standing in front of people to communicate your ideas and get them to agree — you can only rely on yourself for this.

**Emotional management and adaptability.** Technology iterates too fast; what you learn today might be outdated tomorrow. The ability to quickly adjust your mindset, not be anxious or resistant, and find your place determines whether you can keep going in the long run.

**Genuine human-to-human communication.** AI can simulate conversations, but it cannot give a sense of trust. Team cohesion comes from face-to-face communication and shared pressure. These things become rarer and more valuable in an era where AI is getting stronger.

> What will be scarce in the future is not "people who know how to use AI" — that threshold will get lower and lower. What will be scarce are people who can define problems, people who can connect with others, and people who can make decisions in uncertainty.

---

## A Feedback from AI

At the end of the chat, it gave me an observation: **I am better at thinking than doing.**

Indeed. Today's anxiety and reflection are very real, but if these thoughts are not translated into action, they are just spinning in my head.

It advised me to: write things down, chat with people better than myself, do things with uncertain outcomes, and read difficult books.

I think that makes sense. Especially "writing things down" — you think you've figured it out, but once you start writing, you realize there are holes everywhere. **Writing is the best tool for thinking.** This article itself is a start.

---

## Final Thoughts

AI is a tool, not a replacement. Use it to accelerate execution, but don't use it to skip thinking.

The fear is not using AI; the fear is losing the desire to even understand.

Fortunately, I am still thinking about these questions. **It shows the desire is still there.** ✨
